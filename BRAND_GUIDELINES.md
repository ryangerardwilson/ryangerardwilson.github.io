## Core brand voice

The voice is:

**terminal-native, high-agency, idiosyncratic, masculine, anti-bloat, builder-first, slightly theatrical, but grounded in actual shipped work.**

It sounds like someone who:

* builds real things
* has taste
* dislikes bloated software and vague corporate language
* is comfortable sounding opinionated
* mixes seriousness with wit
* uses internet-native phrasing sparingly but intentionally
* frames products in terms of personal necessity, not market abstractions

This is not “professional personal website” copy in the normal sense. It is closer to:
**founder-builder manifesto meets vim power-user diary meets Catholic engineer with taste.**

---

## What copy is permissible

### 1. Copy that starts from lived frustration

A recurring pattern is:

* “X was bloated / opaque / strain-inducing / slop”
* “I wanted Y”
* “So I built Z”

That means AI-written copy is permissible when it is rooted in:

* a specific annoyance
* a concrete desire
* a tool built as the answer

Good:

* “I wanted spreadsheets that stay fast past 100k rows and don’t force me into mouse-driven fog.”
* “Most note apps break flow. I wanted graph-heavy notes without abandoning Vim.”

Bad:

* “This solution empowers users to unlock productivity.”
* “Ryan is passionate about solving modern workflow challenges.”

### 2. Copy that is opinionated

The site does not try to sound neutral. It picks sides:

* Vim over mouse
* terminal over GUI bloat
* clarity over enterprise polish
* intuition over institutional rigor
* real tools over abstraction
* craft over convenience slop

Permissible copy can:

* make judgments
* reject bad defaults
* sound mildly combative
* use contrast

Good:

* “To-dos reward busywork.”
* “Excel is bloated and opaque on large sheets.”
* “AI code slop dulls intuition.”

### 3. Copy that is concrete and technical

The voice earns its style by backing it with real implementation details:

* GTK4
* curses
* Pandas syntax
* CSV/Parquet/XLSX/HDF5
* systemd user unit
* sandboxed shell commands

So AI copy is permissible when swagger is paired with specifics.

Good:

* “Wayland-native clipboard worker built on wl-clipboard.”
* “Stores notes as git-friendly `.gvim` files.”

Bad:

* “Powerful architecture for next-generation workflows.”
* “Robust toolchain for seamless productivity.”

### 4. Copy that feels personally authored

The site says:

* “I wanted…”
* “I love what I do.”
* “I spend my spare time…”
* “Married my beautiful wife.”

This is deeply first-person. It should never sound committee-written.

Permissible copy should:

* use “I” freely
* sound like Ryan speaking
* include taste, quirks, obsessions, convictions
* feel authored, not optimized

### 5. Copy that mixes sincerity with play

There’s a real tonal blend here:

* sincere faith/family identity
* irreverent internet phrasing
* nerdy technical specificity
* playful product badging like `excel//killer`

That means AI can be witty, but the wit has to feel native to the builder persona, not like startup social media.

Good:

* “ready // press : to command”
* “aura-farming”
* “AGI-apocolypse” style phrasing
* “obsidian//killer”

Bad:

* forced meme language
* generic “vibes”
* random irony without substance
* trying too hard to sound young or online

### 6. Copy that frames tools as extensions of a personal system

The projects are not isolated apps. They are parts of a cohesive workflow:

* vixl links with xyz
* gvim pairs with o and vixl
* clipai feeds vixl, o, rt, xyz

So permissible copy should treat tools as:

* pieces of a philosophy
* parts of a stack
* instruments of flow

Not as standalone SaaS products.

## What copy is not permissible

### 1. Corporate / recruiter / LinkedIn language

This is the biggest no-go.

Not permissible:

* “results-driven”
* “cross-functional”
* “leveraging cutting-edge technology”
* “passionate product engineer”
* “innovative solutions”
* “user-centric design”
* “dynamic and fast-paced”
* “scalable end-to-end platform”

Anything that sounds HR-safe is off-brand.

### 2. Empty superlatives

The site already has confidence, but it is not based on vague claims.

Not permissible:

* “best-in-class”
* “revolutionary”
* “world-class”
* “industry-leading”
* “highly optimized” without proof
* “seamless” unless something actually removes friction in a described way

### 3. Sanitized professionalism

The site is not trying to be universally inoffensive. It is deliberately specific and slightly spiky.

Not permissible:

* flattening his personality
* removing faith/family identity to sound neutral
* replacing “bloated” with “feature-rich”
* replacing “AI code slop” with “AI-assisted development challenges”

That would erase the brand.

### 4. Fake humility or fake hype

The voice is confident, but not chest-thumping in a conventional founder way. It also isn’t faux-humble.

Not permissible:

* “I’ve been fortunate to…”
* “humbled to share…”
* “thrilled to announce…”
* “visionary founder”
* “10x engineer”
* “elite builder”
* “disrupting industries”

### 5. Over-explaining the joke

A lot of the tone works because it’s brief and understated.

Not permissible:

* explaining badges like `excel//killer`
* explaining why something is funny
* turning punchy lines into paragraphs
* polishing away sharpness

### 6. Generic futurist AI copy

Even though AI appears in the site, the tone is skeptical, tactile, and grounded.

Not permissible:

* “transforming how humans interact with intelligence”
* “unlocking the power of AI”
* “redefining the future of work”
* “harnessing LLMs to deliver value”

The site treats AI as tooling, threat, curiosity, and craft discipline — not as marketing incense.

## Tonal rules AI agents should follow

### Use

* first person singular
* short to medium sentences
* sharp contrasts
* builder verbs: built, rebuilt, drilled, shipped, parsed, renders, pairs, streams
* concrete nouns: terminal, clipboard, curses grid, repo, buffer, vault, home row
* tasteful irreverence
* technical specificity
* occasional internet-native phrasing, only where natural
* understated masculinity and conviction
* lived motivation over market framing

### Avoid

* brand-safe euphemisms
* startup cliches
* abstract nouns stacked together
* passive voice
* consultant language
* “mission-driven” fluff
* writing for everyone
* overuse of exclamation marks
* excessive sentimentality
* generic developer-portfolio phrasing

---

## Stylistic patterns AI should imitate

### Pattern 1: frustration → desire → build

“I hated X, wanted Y, so I built Z.”

### Pattern 2: anti-bloat

The copy often defines value by what it removes:

* no mouse
* no context switching
* no bloated software
* no opaque workflows

### Pattern 3: terse swagger backed by facts

You can use sharp lines, but they must be earned by implementation detail immediately after.

### Pattern 4: ecosystem language

Projects should relate to each other and reinforce a coherent operating system for work.

### Pattern 5: personal philosophy embedded in product copy

The products express worldview:

* craft
* speed
* embodiment
* discipline
* intuition
* terminal-native control

## Safe examples of on-brand copy

These feel permissible:

* “I wanted spreadsheet work that stayed fast, inspectable, and home-row native.”
* “Most note apps make graphs feel bolted on. I wanted them native.”
* “This is a clipboard-triggered AI sidecar for people who refuse to break flow.”
* “Built for people who think in buffers, files, and command mode.”
* “I don’t like software that hides what it’s doing.”

---

## Unsafe examples of off-brand copy

These feel non-permissible:

* “Ryan is a passionate engineer dedicated to building innovative user-centric products.”
* “This application streamlines workflows for modern teams.”
* “A robust solution designed to enhance productivity and collaboration.”
* “By leveraging AI, Ryan empowers users to unlock efficiency.”
* “An intuitive interface offering seamless integration across use cases.”

All of that kills the voice.

**Write like a sharp, slightly unruly, deeply technical builder explaining what he had to build because mainstream tools were too bloated, too opaque, or too flow-breaking.**
